# Expert Track — PM Career Framework (August 2023)

> Reference document for the Product Coach skill. Contains the complete IC leveling matrix from Spotify's PM Career Framework.

---

## Level Overview

| Level | Title | Workday Level | Short Description | Business Need for Promotion | Expected Time in Role |
|-------|-------|---------------|-------------------|-----------------------------|-----------------------|
| 1 | Associate Product Manager | Level K | New college graduate with limited to no experience in a PM role. Often working side-by-side with a more experienced PM. They're committed to learn how to be an autonomous PM. | Not required | 9-15 months |
| 2 | Product Manager I | Level J | An early-career IC who has some experience in a PM role or a related role. | Yes | 9-15 months |
| 3 | Product Manager II | Level I | An IC that can confidently manage the scope of a squad, delivering end user value consistently while facing some strategic ambiguity. | Yes | N/A |
| 4 | Senior Product Manager | Level H | An experienced IC that can confidently manage a complex or large scope, delivering value against multi-team objectives in the face of strategic ambiguity. | Yes | N/A |
| 5 | Principal Product Manager | Level G | A very experienced IC that can confidently manage a complex or large scope, delivering value against the objectives of a studio with little strategic direction. | Yes | N/A |
| 6 | Senior Principal | Level F | An expert IC that can confidently manage a complex or large scope, delivering value of a mission with little strategic direction. | Yes | N/A |

## Criteria

### 1. Portfolio Size & Complexity

**Category:** Product Scope | **Type:** Achievement

| Level 1 — APM | Level 2 — PM I | Level 3 — PM II | Level 4 — Senior PM | Level 5 — Principal PM | Level 6 — Senior Principal |
|----------------|----------------|-----------------|---------------------|------------------------|---------------------------|
| Responsible for clearly defined tasks within a specific project | Responsible for a feature or specific project to improve an existing feature | Responsible for a set of features or a product | Responsible for a complex product with several configurations, features, functions and flows that produce a distinct outcome | Responsible for a very complex product or program spanning multiple product teams, configurations, features, functions, flows and/or outcomes | Responsible for a product or set of products with the highest-degree of complexity (often requiring exceptional domain expertise) or a large program spanning many teams. |

#### In artifacts, look for

- **Scope definition:** Does the PM clearly articulate what they own? Is it a task, a feature, a product, or a portfolio?
- **Cross-team dependencies:** Are dependencies identified and managed, or is work self-contained within one squad?
- **Configuration complexity:** Does the product involve multiple configurations, user flows, or system states that the PM must reason about?
- **Number of features or products mentioned:** PRDs covering a single feature vs. PRDs orchestrating multiple features into a coherent product outcome.
- **Domain expertise signals:** Does the PM demonstrate deep understanding of the domain (financial systems, content delivery, marketplace dynamics) or treat the domain as interchangeable?
- **System-level reasoning:** Does the PM reason about how their product interacts with adjacent products, or do they treat their scope as isolated?

#### Calibration helper

| Bracket | Below Level | At Level | Above Level |
|---------|-------------|----------|-------------|
| **Levels 1-2** | Needs step-by-step guidance on tasks; does not independently identify what work belongs to them. Scope is unclear or undefined in artifacts. | APM completes defined tasks within a project. PM I owns a feature end-to-end — from definition through delivery — with clear boundaries. PRDs describe a specific feature with known inputs and outputs. | PM I proactively identifies adjacent features that should be bundled. Begins reasoning about how their feature fits into the broader product. Scope definition in PRDs goes beyond the assigned feature. |
| **Level 3** | Owns a single feature but struggles to manage a set of features simultaneously. PRDs treat each feature as independent rather than part of a coherent product. Dependencies are discovered reactively. | Owns a set of features or a product. PRDs articulate how features relate to each other. Status updates cover multiple workstreams. Stakeholder communication reflects awareness of the full product scope. | Begins managing cross-team scope. Identifies complexity across product boundaries. PRDs reference system-level interactions beyond their squad's direct ownership. |
| **Level 4** | Manages multiple features but does not demonstrate coherent product-level thinking. Treats configurations and flows as separate workstreams rather than a unified product. | Owns a complex product with multiple configurations, features, functions, and flows that produce a distinct outcome. Artifacts show how pieces fit together. Decision logs reflect trade-offs across the full product surface. | Proactively identifies program-level scope opportunities. Begins coordinating across multiple product teams without being asked. Artifacts demonstrate reasoning about outcomes across team boundaries. |
| **Levels 5-6** | Manages a complex product but scope remains within a single team boundary. Does not demonstrate program-level thinking or multi-team coordination. | Principal PM owns a program spanning multiple product teams with complex configurations and outcomes. Senior Principal owns the highest-complexity products or largest programs, often requiring exceptional domain expertise. Artifacts reflect portfolio-level reasoning. | Senior Principal shapes the scope definition itself — redefining boundaries, creating new product categories, or restructuring how teams think about their product areas. |

### 2. Roadmap Horizon

**Category:** Product Scope | **Type:** Achievement

| Level 1 — APM | Level 2 — PM I | Level 3 — PM II | Level 4 — Senior PM | Level 5 — Principal PM | Level 6 — Senior Principal |
|----------------|----------------|-----------------|---------------------|------------------------|---------------------------|
| Learns to create and track progress of goals for a specific project | Works towards bi-annual goals. | Works towards bi-annual goals with subsequent time-periods in mind. | Works towards bi-annual and annual objectives. | Works towards bi-annual and annual objectives across squads in a studio or program. | Works towards bi-annual, annual and multi-year objectives across squads in a mission or large program. |

#### In artifacts, look for

- **Time horizon references:** Do roadmaps and PRDs reference only the current half, or do they articulate what comes next?
- **Goal framing:** Are goals framed as project completions ("ship X") or as outcome milestones ("achieve Y metric by H2")?
- **Sequential thinking:** Does the PM articulate how current work sets up future work? Are there explicit "Phase 1 / Phase 2" framings?
- **Multi-team coordination timeline:** For senior levels, do roadmaps account for dependencies across squads with different timelines?
- **Strategic context connection:** Does the roadmap connect upward to mission or company objectives, or is it purely tactical?
- **Checkpoint design:** Are there intermediate milestones that allow for course correction, or is the roadmap a linear march to a single deadline?

#### Calibration helper

| Bracket | Below Level | At Level | Above Level |
|---------|-------------|----------|-------------|
| **Levels 1-2** | Cannot articulate project goals or track progress without significant guidance. No written goals or milestones in artifacts. | APM creates and tracks goals for their assigned project. PM I works toward bi-annual goals with clear milestones and progress tracking. Roadmaps exist and are maintained. | PM I begins referencing what happens after the current half. Status updates include "next half" thinking. Goals are framed as stepping stones rather than endpoints. |
| **Level 3** | Works toward bi-annual goals but treats them as isolated. No evidence of thinking beyond the current planning period. Roadmaps end at the half boundary. | Works toward bi-annual goals with subsequent time-periods in mind. Roadmaps include a "what comes next" section. PRDs articulate how current work enables future work. Status updates reference both current progress and future setup. | Begins articulating annual objectives. Roadmaps show two or more planning periods. Proactively identifies work that should start now to enable future-half goals. |
| **Level 4** | Manages bi-annual goals but annual objectives are vague or absent. Roadmap does not connect current-half work to a longer arc. | Works toward bi-annual and annual objectives. Roadmaps span at least a full year with clear phase gates. Decision docs reference annual strategy. Status updates frame current work in the context of annual goals. | Begins coordinating roadmap timelines across multiple squads. Identifies cross-team sequencing dependencies. Annual roadmap includes contingency paths. |
| **Levels 5-6** | Manages annual objectives for a single team but does not coordinate across squads or programs. Roadmap does not reflect multi-team dependencies. | Principal PM coordinates bi-annual and annual objectives across squads in a studio or program. Senior Principal works toward multi-year objectives across a mission, with roadmaps that span multiple years and account for organizational evolution. | Senior Principal influences company-level multi-year planning. Roadmaps shape how the organization thinks about long-term product direction. Creates frameworks that other teams use for their own roadmapping. |

### 3. Ambiguity

**Category:** Product Strategy | **Type:** Competence

| Level 1 — APM | Level 2 — PM I | Level 3 — PM II | Level 4 — Senior PM | Level 5 — Principal PM | Level 6 — Senior Principal |
|----------------|----------------|-----------------|---------------------|------------------------|---------------------------|
| Supports execution of known solutions for a specific project | Execution of known solutions or simple problems with low strategic ambiguity | Given a problem with some strategic ambiguity, can lead product discovery and prioritize solutions | Given a broader problem with strategic ambiguity, can break it down into multiple problems and prioritize them for discovery | Given a very large and unexplored problem with high strategic ambiguity, can break it down into multiple problems and prioritize them for discovery | Given a very large and unexplored problem with no strategic guidance, can break it down into multiple problems and prioritize them for discovery |

#### In artifacts, look for

- **Problem framing:** Is the problem pre-defined (given to the PM) or did the PM define it themselves? How much structure existed before they started?
- **Assumption surfacing:** Does the PM call out what is known, unknown, and assumed? Are assumptions testable?
- **Discovery approach:** Is there evidence of structured discovery — user research, data analysis, prototyping — or does the PM jump to solutions?
- **Problem decomposition:** Does the PM break large ambiguous problems into smaller, sequenced sub-problems?
- **Confidence signaling:** Does the PM distinguish between confirmed facts, reported information, and inferred conclusions?
- **Kill signals:** Does the PM define conditions under which they would stop pursuing a direction?
- **Hypothesis structure:** Are hypotheses explicit, testable, and connected to evidence? Or are they unstated assumptions embedded in PRDs?

#### Calibration helper

| Bracket | Below Level | At Level | Above Level |
|---------|-------------|----------|-------------|
| **Levels 1-2** | Cannot execute on known solutions without step-by-step direction. Struggles when any element is undefined. Asks "what should I do?" rather than "here's what I plan to do." | APM supports execution of known solutions. PM I executes known solutions or tackles simple problems with low ambiguity. Artifacts show clear problem-to-solution mapping for well-defined problems. | PM I begins leading discovery on simple problems. Proposes multiple solution options for straightforward problems. Identifies when a problem is less defined than initially presented. |
| **Level 3** | Executes known solutions well but freezes or escalates when facing ambiguity. Does not lead discovery independently. PRDs assume the solution is already known. | Given a problem with some strategic ambiguity, leads product discovery and prioritizes solutions. Artifacts show explicit hypothesis-testing. Problem frames acknowledge unknowns. Decision logs show options evaluated and rationale documented. | Begins decomposing broader problems without prompting. Identifies adjacent problems that should be explored. Proactively surfaces ambiguity to stakeholders rather than waiting for it to be discovered. |
| **Level 4** | Handles ambiguity within a single problem but struggles to decompose broader problems. Tends to pick a solution too early rather than exploring the problem space. | Given a broader problem with strategic ambiguity, breaks it down into multiple problems and prioritizes them for discovery. Problem frames show structured decomposition. Roadmaps reflect a discovery-then-delivery sequence. Prioritization rationale is explicit. | Begins tackling very large, unexplored problems. Creates frameworks for others to use in navigating ambiguity. Shapes how the team thinks about the problem space. |
| **Levels 5-6** | Decomposes problems but relies on strategic direction from leadership. Does not independently define the problem space or set strategic direction. | Principal PM navigates very large, unexplored problems with high strategic ambiguity. Senior Principal operates with no strategic guidance, defining the problem space and strategic direction themselves. Artifacts show novel frameworks for understanding new domains. | Senior Principal redefines how the company thinks about entire problem categories. Creates strategic clarity where none existed. Other teams adopt their problem framing. |

### 4. Roadmapping

**Category:** Product Strategy | **Type:** Competence

| Level 1 — APM | Level 2 — PM I | Level 3 — PM II | Level 4 — Senior PM | Level 5 — Principal PM | Level 6 — Senior Principal |
|----------------|----------------|-----------------|---------------------|------------------------|---------------------------|
| Learns to produce actionable roadmaps for executing on a specific project | Produces actionable and strategically sequenced roadmaps for executing on work within a project | Produces actionable and strategically sequenced roadmaps for work within a squad | Produces actionable and strategically sequenced roadmaps for a complex product or portfolio of multiple products | Produces an actionable and strategically sequenced roadmap for a very complex product or program | Produces an actionable and strategically sequenced roadmap for the most complex products or largest programs |

#### In artifacts, look for

- **Sequencing rationale:** Does the roadmap explain why items are ordered the way they are? Is sequencing strategic (dependency-driven, risk-reducing, value-maximizing) or arbitrary?
- **Actionability:** Can an engineering team pick up roadmap items and start work, or are items vague aspirations?
- **Scope coverage:** Does the roadmap cover the full scope of the PM's responsibility, or does it focus on one thread while ignoring others?
- **Dependency mapping:** Are cross-team or cross-system dependencies identified and accounted for in timing?
- **Phase gates and checkpoints:** Are there explicit points where the team will reassess, pivot, or kill work?
- **Resource awareness:** Does the roadmap account for team capacity, or does it assume infinite resources?

#### Calibration helper

| Bracket | Below Level | At Level | Above Level |
|---------|-------------|----------|-------------|
| **Levels 1-2** | Cannot produce a project plan without heavy guidance. Tasks are listed but not sequenced or connected to outcomes. | APM learns to produce actionable roadmaps for a specific project. PM I produces actionable, strategically sequenced roadmaps within a project. Items have clear owners, timelines, and dependencies. | PM I produces roadmaps that account for squad-level context beyond their project. Begins identifying how their project roadmap interacts with other squad priorities. |
| **Level 3** | Produces project-level roadmaps but struggles to sequence work across a full squad's scope. Roadmap items are isolated rather than strategically ordered. | Produces actionable, strategically sequenced roadmaps for work within a squad. Sequencing rationale is documented. Dependencies within the squad are mapped. Roadmap reflects trade-offs between competing priorities. | Begins producing roadmaps that account for cross-squad dependencies. Identifies when squad roadmap items depend on or enable work in other teams. |
| **Level 4** | Produces squad-level roadmaps but struggles with portfolio-level coordination. Does not account for how multiple products interact or sequence against each other. | Produces actionable, strategically sequenced roadmaps for a complex product or portfolio of multiple products. Dependencies across products are mapped. Sequencing reflects strategic priority across the portfolio. Resource allocation is considered. | Begins producing program-level roadmaps. Identifies cross-studio dependencies. Creates roadmap frameworks that other PMs adopt for their own planning. |
| **Levels 5-6** | Manages portfolio-level roadmaps but does not coordinate at program or mission scale. Dependencies beyond the immediate portfolio are not accounted for. | Principal PM produces roadmaps for very complex products or programs with multiple workstreams. Senior Principal produces roadmaps for the most complex products or largest programs, often spanning an entire mission. Roadmaps shape organizational planning cadence. | Senior Principal's roadmaps influence company-level planning. Creates sequencing frameworks adopted across multiple missions. Roadmapping methodology becomes a reference for the PM community. |

### 5. Trade-offs

**Category:** Product Strategy | **Type:** Competence

| Level 1 — APM | Level 2 — PM I | Level 3 — PM II | Level 4 — Senior PM | Level 5 — Principal PM | Level 6 — Senior Principal |
|----------------|----------------|-----------------|---------------------|------------------------|---------------------------|
| Learns to identify trade-offs for a specific project | Can identify trade-offs for projects that achieve the right balance of impact and effort | Makes trade-offs between multiple paths to impact for a team's objectives | Makes trade-offs between multiple team objectives that can impact the organization's goals | Drives towards an informed decision when facing very complex trade-offs that can impact the organization's goals | Drives towards an informed decision when facing trade-offs with the highest degree of complexity that can impact the company's goals |

#### In artifacts, look for

- **Explicit option comparison:** Does the PM present multiple options with pros/cons, or do they present a single recommendation without alternatives?
- **Documented rationale:** Is the "why" behind the chosen path clearly articulated? Can a reader understand what was considered and rejected?
- **Impact-effort reasoning:** Does the PM explicitly weigh impact against effort, cost, and risk?
- **Stakeholder consideration:** Does the trade-off analysis account for different stakeholder perspectives and priorities?
- **Reversibility assessment:** Does the PM distinguish between reversible and irreversible decisions, adjusting rigor accordingly?
- **Opportunity cost framing:** Does the PM articulate what is NOT being done as a result of the chosen path?
- **Decision escalation judgment:** Does the PM know when a trade-off exceeds their authority and needs escalation?

#### Calibration helper

| Bracket | Below Level | At Level | Above Level |
|---------|-------------|----------|-------------|
| **Levels 1-2** | Cannot identify trade-offs without guidance. Presents solutions without considering alternatives. Does not articulate what is being traded away. | APM learns to identify trade-offs within a project. PM I identifies trade-offs that balance impact and effort within projects. Decision docs show at least two options considered with a rationale for the chosen path. | PM I begins identifying trade-offs that affect the broader team, not just the project. Proactively raises trade-offs to their manager rather than discovering them after the fact. |
| **Level 3** | Identifies trade-offs within a project but struggles to reason across a team's full set of objectives. Tends to optimize locally without considering team-level impact. | Makes trade-offs between multiple paths to impact for the team's objectives. Decision logs show structured option comparison. PRDs include explicit non-goals. Prioritization frameworks are used and documented. | Begins identifying trade-offs that affect organizational goals beyond the team. Raises cross-team trade-offs proactively. Decision framing includes stakeholder perspectives from outside the immediate team. |
| **Level 4** | Makes team-level trade-offs but does not connect them to organizational goals. Trade-off analysis stays within squad boundaries. | Makes trade-offs between multiple team objectives that impact organizational goals. Decision docs show cross-team impact analysis. Trade-off framing includes organizational context. Stakeholder alignment is actively managed. | Begins driving complex trade-offs that affect the studio or program. Creates decision frameworks that help other PMs navigate similar trade-offs. Trade-off resolution influences organizational strategy. |
| **Levels 5-6** | Makes organization-level trade-offs but relies on leadership to frame the decision space. Does not independently identify the highest-leverage trade-offs. | Principal PM drives informed decisions on very complex trade-offs affecting organizational goals. Senior Principal drives decisions on trade-offs with the highest degree of complexity affecting company goals. Trade-off resolution creates precedent. | Senior Principal's trade-off decisions shape company strategy. Creates frameworks for trade-off analysis adopted beyond their organization. Reframes trade-offs to reveal structural solutions that eliminate the trade-off entirely. |

### 6. Trust & Influence

**Category:** Leadership | **Type:** Behavior

| Level 1 — APM | Level 2 — PM I | Level 3 — PM II | Level 4 — Senior PM | Level 5 — Principal PM | Level 6 — Senior Principal |
|----------------|----------------|-----------------|---------------------|------------------------|---------------------------|
| Develops relationships with XFN counterparts on projects within a squad | Fosters strong relationships with XFN counterparts on projects within a squad | Trusted to lead a high performing squad and manages stakeholders throughout delivery | Trusted to lead workstreams with multiple squad(s), dependencies, and more complex stakeholder networks | Trusted to provide leadership to a high-priority program with multiple workstreams, complex dependencies, and complex stakeholder networks | Trusted to lead high-priority program with multiple workstreams and the highest complexity of dependencies and stakeholder networks |

#### In artifacts, look for

- **Stakeholder mapping:** Does the PM identify who needs to be aligned, informed, or consulted? Is the stakeholder network documented?
- **Influence evidence:** Are there signs that the PM's recommendations are adopted? Do decision logs show the PM driving alignment rather than being directed?
- **XFN relationship signals:** Do artifacts reference input from design, engineering, data, legal, finance — or is the PM working in isolation?
- **Dependency management:** Does the PM proactively manage cross-team dependencies, or are they discovered late?
- **Escalation judgment:** Does the PM escalate appropriately — neither too early (lacking confidence) nor too late (blocking progress)?
- **Stakeholder communication cadence:** Is there evidence of regular, structured communication with stakeholders (weekly updates, review meetings, async syncs)?

#### Calibration helper

| Bracket | Below Level | At Level | Above Level |
|---------|-------------|----------|-------------|
| **Levels 1-2** | Does not build relationships with XFN counterparts. Works in isolation or only through their manager. Stakeholders do not know who they are. | APM develops relationships with XFN counterparts within a squad. PM I fosters strong relationships — design, engineering, data partners seek their input and trust their judgment on projects. | PM I is sought out by XFN partners for input beyond their assigned projects. Begins building relationships outside the squad. Stakeholders proactively share context with them. |
| **Level 3** | Has relationships with XFN counterparts but is not trusted to lead the squad independently. Stakeholders route around them to their manager. Delivery requires manager intervention to unblock. | Trusted to lead a high-performing squad and manage stakeholders throughout delivery. The squad looks to them for direction. Stakeholders engage with them directly. Decision-making authority is clear and respected. | Begins leading workstreams that involve multiple squads. Is sought out by PMs and leaders outside their squad for advice. Stakeholders from adjacent teams proactively align with them. |
| **Level 4** | Leads a squad effectively but struggles with multi-squad coordination. Dependencies are managed reactively. Stakeholder network is limited to immediate team boundaries. | Trusted to lead workstreams with multiple squads, dependencies, and complex stakeholder networks. Cross-team alignment happens through them. Dependency management is proactive. Stakeholders at multiple levels trust their judgment. | Begins providing leadership to high-priority programs. Is trusted by senior leadership with high-visibility, high-stakes work. Influence extends beyond their direct scope. |
| **Levels 5-6** | Leads multi-squad workstreams but is not trusted with the highest-priority programs. Stakeholder network does not extend to senior leadership. Complex dependencies require escalation. | Principal PM provides leadership to high-priority programs with multiple workstreams and complex dependencies. Senior Principal leads programs with the highest complexity of dependencies and stakeholder networks. Leadership trusts them to represent the company's interests. | Senior Principal's influence shapes organizational structure. Is a go-to advisor for senior leadership on cross-cutting decisions. Creates stakeholder alignment frameworks adopted by others. |

### 7. Communication

**Category:** Leadership | **Type:** Behavior

| Level 1 — APM | Level 2 — PM I | Level 3 — PM II | Level 4 — Senior PM | Level 5 — Principal PM | Level 6 — Senior Principal |
|----------------|----------------|-----------------|---------------------|------------------------|---------------------------|
| Learns to communicate context and vision to team members for a specific project | Effective in communicating context, vision, and strategy to participants in a project | Effective in communicating context, vision, and strategy to a squad | Effective in communicating context, vision, and strategy to squad(s) and stakeholders | Effective in communicating context, vision, and strategy to program participants and stakeholders | Effective in communicating context, vision, and strategy to program participants, stakeholders, and the company |

#### In artifacts, look for

- **Audience awareness:** Does the PM tailor communication to the audience? Engineering-facing docs vs. exec updates vs. stakeholder briefs should look different.
- **Clarity of framing:** Can a reader understand the core message in the first paragraph? Is the "so what" clear?
- **Strategic narrative:** Does the PM connect tactical work to strategic context? Do status updates tell a story, or are they lists of tasks?
- **Conciseness:** Is communication appropriately brief? Senior PMs should communicate more complex ideas in fewer words, not more.
- **Visual communication:** Does the PM use diagrams, tables, and structured formats to improve comprehension?
- **Proactive communication:** Does the PM push updates out, or do stakeholders have to pull information?
- **Uncertainty communication:** Does the PM communicate what they don't know as clearly as what they do know?

#### Calibration helper

| Bracket | Below Level | At Level | Above Level |
|---------|-------------|----------|-------------|
| **Levels 1-2** | Communication is unclear or requires significant interpretation. Does not provide context for why work matters. Team members are confused about project direction. | APM learns to communicate context and vision for a specific project. PM I effectively communicates context, vision, and strategy to project participants. Updates are clear, concise, and actionable. | PM I tailors communication to different audiences within the project. Begins proactively sharing context rather than waiting to be asked. Written artifacts are well-structured and easy to follow. |
| **Level 3** | Communicates within projects but struggles to provide squad-level context. Updates focus on task completion rather than strategic progress. Squad members do not have a clear picture of direction. | Effectively communicates context, vision, and strategy to a squad. The squad understands why they are working on what they are working on. Status updates connect work to objectives. Written artifacts are audience-appropriate. | Begins communicating effectively beyond the squad. Stakeholders outside the team understand the squad's direction and progress. Communication creates alignment without requiring meetings. |
| **Level 4** | Communicates well within a squad but struggles when the audience expands to multiple squads and stakeholders. Messages do not land with stakeholders outside the immediate team. | Effectively communicates context, vision, and strategy to multiple squads and stakeholders. Exec updates are concise and decision-oriented. Stakeholder briefs are tailored to the audience. Strategic narrative is coherent across artifacts. | Begins communicating at program or studio level. Creates communication frameworks that other PMs adopt. Narrative framing influences how stakeholders think about the problem space. |
| **Levels 5-6** | Communicates well to multi-squad audiences but does not reach program-level or company-level stakeholders. Communication does not shape broader organizational thinking. | Principal PM communicates effectively to program participants and stakeholders. Senior Principal communicates to the company — their narratives shape how the organization understands a problem domain. Communication creates clarity and alignment at scale. | Senior Principal's communication influences company culture and product thinking. Creates communication standards adopted across the PM community. Their framing of problems becomes the default framing. |

### 9. Scale of Impact

**Category:** Impact | **Type:** Achievement

| Level 1 — APM | Level 2 — PM I | Level 3 — PM II | Level 4 — Senior PM | Level 5 — Principal PM | Level 6 — Senior Principal |
|----------------|----------------|-----------------|---------------------|------------------------|---------------------------|
| Completes the work they are assigned for a specific project | Delivers value on projects for internal or external customers that contribute to the team(s) objectives (e.g. metrics, enablement, performance) | Delivers high-value projects for internal or external customers that contribute to the team(s) objectives (e.g. metrics, enablement, performance) | Track record of delivering sustainable and high-value/performing products for internal or external customers across multiple complex products that meets the team's objectives | Track record of delivering sustainable and high-value/performing priority products for internal or external customers across multiple complex products that meets objectives that often involve multiple teams in a program | Track record of delivering sustainable and high-value/performing products against the companies highest-priority initiatives |

#### In artifacts, look for

- **Outcome measurement:** Does the PM measure impact in terms of outcomes (metrics moved, user behavior changed, business value created) or outputs (features shipped, tickets closed)?
- **Sustainability signals:** Is the impact one-time or sustained? Does the PM build products that continue to deliver value after launch?
- **Value framing:** Does the PM articulate the value delivered in terms the business cares about (revenue, retention, efficiency, risk reduction)?
- **Track record evidence:** Is there a pattern of delivery over time, or is evidence limited to a single project?
- **Customer impact:** Does the PM connect product decisions to customer outcomes — whether internal or external?
- **Metric ownership:** Does the PM own and track success metrics, or do they ship features and move on?
- **High-value distinction:** At PM II and above, is the work clearly high-value (strategic, differentiated, measurable) rather than incremental?

#### Calibration helper

| Bracket | Below Level | At Level | Above Level |
|---------|-------------|----------|-------------|
| **Levels 1-2** | Does not complete assigned work independently. Work requires significant rework or does not contribute to team objectives. Impact is not measurable. | APM completes assigned work for a specific project. | **Level 3** | Delivers projects but they are not clearly high-value. Impact is incremental rather than strategic. Metrics are not owned or tracked. | Delivers high-value projects that contribute to team objectives. Impact is measurable, strategic, and connected to business outcomes. Success metrics are defined, tracked, and reported. There is a pattern of delivery, not just a single success. | Begins delivering impact across multiple products or teams. Track record extends beyond the current half. Impact is sustainable — products continue to deliver value after initial launch. |
| **Level 4** | Delivers high-value projects but impact does not extend across multiple complex products. Track record is limited to a single product. Impact is not yet sustainable at portfolio scale. | Track record of delivering sustainable, high-value products across multiple complex products that meet team objectives. Impact is consistent over time. Products perform well after launch. Delivery quality builds organizational trust. | Begins delivering impact at program level. Products are recognized as priority initiatives. Impact influences objectives beyond the immediate team. |
| **Levels 5-6** | Delivers across multiple products but is not associated with the highest-priority programs. Track record does not demonstrate program-level impact. | Principal PM has a track record of delivering high-value priority products that meet multi-team objectives. Senior Principal delivers against the company's highest-priority initiatives. Impact is recognized at the organizational and company level. | Senior Principal's impact defines what "highest-priority" means. Delivery creates new categories of value. Track record is referenced as exemplary across the company. |
