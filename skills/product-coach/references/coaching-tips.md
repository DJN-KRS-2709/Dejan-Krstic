# Coaching Tips — Industry Best Practices

> Reference document for the Product Coach skill. Curated frameworks and advice from leading PM thought leaders, organized by career framework criterion.

---

## 1. Portfolio Size & Complexity

### 1a. Opportunity Solution Trees (Teresa Torres)

**Source:** Teresa Torres, *Continuous Discovery Habits* (2021)

As portfolio complexity grows, PMs need a structured way to connect business outcomes to the discovery work happening across multiple product areas. The Opportunity Solution Tree (OST) maps a desired outcome at the top, branches into opportunity spaces (customer needs, pain points, desires), and then maps solution ideas and assumption tests beneath each opportunity. This creates a visual inventory of the entire problem space a PM owns.

**By level:**
- **Level 1-2:** Use OSTs to map a single feature area. Demonstrates you understand the problem space, not just the backlog.
- **Level 3:** Maintain OSTs across 2-3 product areas simultaneously. Show how opportunities in one area interact with or cannibalize another.
- **Level 4-5:** Use OSTs to communicate portfolio-level strategy to leadership. The tree itself becomes the artifact that shows why you are investing where you are, and what you are deliberately not pursuing.
- **Level 6:** OSTs span entire product lines. You are coaching other PMs to maintain their own trees and ensuring they connect to a unified strategic outcome.

### 1b. The LNO Framework (Shreyas Doshi)

**Source:** Shreyas Doshi, Twitter/X threads and conference talks on PM effectiveness

Shreyas Doshi's LNO framework classifies all PM work into three categories: **Leverage** (high-impact work that compounds), **Neutral** (necessary work that maintains the status quo), and **Overhead** (work that must be done but creates no direct value). The insight is that most PMs spend 70%+ of their time on Neutral and Overhead work, and the path to greater portfolio complexity is not doing more work but shifting the ratio toward Leverage tasks.

**By level:**
- **Level 1-2:** Learn to identify which of your tasks are L, N, or O. Start tracking your weekly time split. The goal is awareness, not perfection.
- **Level 3:** Actively manage your LNO ratio. Delegate or automate Overhead. Batch Neutral work. Protect 40%+ of your time for Leverage activities. This is how you create capacity for more complex portfolio ownership.
- **Level 4-5:** Apply LNO thinking to your entire team's portfolio. Help engineers and designers recognize their own Leverage work. The PM's job shifts from doing Leverage work to creating the conditions for Leverage work to happen across the team.
- **Level 6:** LNO becomes an organizational design tool. You are restructuring teams, processes, and rituals to maximize Leverage work across a product org.

### 1c. Empowered Teams vs Feature Teams (Marty Cagan)

**Source:** Marty Cagan, *Empowered* (2020), Silicon Valley Product Group

Cagan draws a sharp distinction between "feature teams" (teams that exist to ship features from a roadmap handed down by stakeholders) and "empowered product teams" (teams given problems to solve with accountability for outcomes). As portfolio complexity grows, the PM's ability to operate as an empowered team leader — not a feature project manager — becomes the differentiator. Empowered PMs own outcomes, discover solutions, and are accountable for results.

**By level:**
- **Level 1-2:** Recognize when you are operating as a feature team PM (taking orders) vs an empowered PM (owning problems). Start advocating for outcome-based goals even within a narrow scope.
- **Level 3:** Consistently operate as an empowered PM. You define the problems worth solving, run discovery, and present solutions — not just execute what stakeholders request.
- **Level 4-5:** You are building empowered teams. You coach other PMs and engineers into empowered behavior. You push back on feature-factory dynamics at the org level.
- **Level 6:** You are designing the organizational structure that makes empowered teams the default, not the exception.

## 2. Roadmap Horizon

### 2a. Working Backwards (Amazon / General Industry)

**Source:** Colin Bryar & Bill Carr, *Working Backwards* (2021); widely adopted across the industry

The Working Backwards method starts with the desired future customer experience and works backward to define what must be built. You write a future press release or customer FAQ describing the product as if it already exists, then identify the gaps between that future state and today. This forces long-horizon thinking because you must articulate a compelling end state before decomposing it into quarterly milestones.

**By level:**
- **Level 1-2:** Practice writing a "future press release" for your current feature work. Even for a small scope, articulating the end state clarifies what you are actually building toward.
- **Level 3:** Use Working Backwards to define 6-12 month product visions. The press release becomes a communication artifact that aligns your team and stakeholders on where you are headed.
- **Level 4-5:** Working Backwards defines multi-year product strategy. You write press releases for 2-3 year outcomes and use them to derive annual roadmaps. The artifact is shared with leadership to secure long-term investment.
- **Level 6:** You are using Working Backwards at the portfolio or business-unit level, defining company-level outcomes and coaching PMs to derive their roadmaps from the shared vision.

### 2b. DHM Model — Strategy Through Time (Gibson Biddle)

**Source:** Gibson Biddle, "How to Define Your Product Strategy" essay series (2019)

Gibson Biddle's DHM model evaluates product strategies on three dimensions: **Delight** (does it meaningfully improve the customer experience?), **Hard-to-copy** (does it create a durable competitive advantage?), and **Margin-enhancing** (does it improve unit economics?). The power of DHM for roadmap horizon is that it separates short-term delighters (which may not be hard-to-copy or margin-enhancing) from long-term strategic bets. A 6-month roadmap might be full of Delight features; a 2-year roadmap must include Hard-to-copy and Margin-enhancing investments.

**By level:**
- **Level 1-2:** Evaluate your current work against DHM. Is it primarily Delight? That is appropriate for your level, but start noticing which work contributes to Hard-to-copy or Margin-enhancing outcomes.
- **Level 3:** Your roadmap should include at least one Hard-to-copy or Margin-enhancing initiative alongside Delight work. You can articulate why certain investments pay off over 6-12 months, not just this quarter.
- **Level 4-5:** You are balancing DHM across a multi-year roadmap. You can explain to leadership how near-term Delight work funds the runway for long-term Hard-to-copy investments.
- **Level 6:** You are defining the DHM strategy for an entire product line or business unit, and coaching PMs to align their roadmaps to the strategic DHM balance.

### 2c. Product Vision vs Product Strategy (Marty Cagan)

**Source:** Marty Cagan, *Inspired* (2017), Silicon Valley Product Group

Cagan distinguishes between **product vision** (the future you are trying to create, 3-10 year horizon) and **product strategy** (the sequence of steps to get there, 1-3 year horizon). Many PMs conflate the two or skip vision entirely, jumping straight to quarterly roadmaps. The vision is the "why" and "where"; the strategy is the "how" and "in what order." Without a vision, roadmaps become reactive lists of stakeholder requests.

**By level:**
- **Level 1-2:** You should be able to articulate your team's product vision in 2-3 sentences. If you cannot, that is a signal to ask your manager or senior PM.
- **Level 3:** You contribute to defining the product vision for your area. Your roadmap is visibly derived from a stated vision and strategy, not just a prioritized backlog.
- **Level 4-5:** You own the product vision for your domain. You can present a compelling 2-3 year vision to leadership and show how the current roadmap is the first chapter of that story.
- **Level 6:** You are setting product vision at the business-unit level and ensuring all domain visions ladder up coherently.

## 3. Ambiguity

### 3a. Pre-mortems (Shreyas Doshi)

**Source:** Shreyas Doshi, "The Art of the Pre-Mortem" (various talks and threads, 2020-2023)

A pre-mortem asks: "Imagine this project has failed spectacularly. What went wrong?" Unlike a post-mortem, a pre-mortem happens before execution. Shreyas argues that pre-mortems are the single highest-leverage activity a PM can do because they surface hidden risks, unspoken assumptions, and political landmines when there is still time to change course. The exercise works precisely because ambiguity is high — it forces the team to name the things that are uncertain rather than pretending they will resolve themselves.

**By level:**
- **Level 1-2:** Run a pre-mortem for any project with more than 2 weeks of engineering work. Even a simple 30-minute exercise with your team surfaces risks you had not considered.
- **Level 3:** Pre-mortems become a standard part of your project kickoff process. You facilitate them skillfully, drawing out concerns from quieter team members and converting vague worries into specific, testable risks.
- **Level 4-5:** You use pre-mortems strategically — not just for projects, but for organizational decisions, team restructurings, and strategy pivots. You can run a pre-mortem with senior leadership and make it productive rather than political.
- **Level 6:** You have institutionalized pre-mortems as an organizational habit. Teams across your org run them routinely, and you coach PMs on how to facilitate them effectively.

### 3b. Assumption Testing / Riskiest Assumption Test (Teresa Torres)

**Source:** Teresa Torres, *Continuous Discovery Habits* (2021)

Torres argues that in high-ambiguity environments, the PM's job is not to eliminate uncertainty through analysis but to identify the riskiest assumptions and test them as cheaply and quickly as possible. The Riskiest Assumption Test (RAT) asks: "What is the single assumption that, if wrong, would make this entire initiative worthless?" Then you design the smallest possible experiment to test that assumption. This is fundamentally different from building an MVP — it is testing a belief, not shipping a product.

**By level:**
- **Level 1-2:** For every feature you are building, write down the top 3 assumptions. Identify which one is riskiest. Discuss with your team whether you could test it before building the full solution.
- **Level 3:** You routinely run assumption tests before committing engineering resources. You can distinguish between desirability assumptions (will users want this?), viability assumptions (can the business sustain this?), feasibility assumptions (can we build this?), and usability assumptions (can users figure this out?).
- **Level 4-5:** You structure entire program roadmaps around assumption testing. Each phase of work is designed to de-risk the next. You can explain to leadership why spending 2 weeks on a test saves 2 months of potentially wasted engineering.
- **Level 6:** You are building a culture of assumption testing across the org. Discovery is not a phase — it is a continuous discipline embedded in how every team operates.

### 3c. Separating Problems from Solutions (Marty Cagan)

**Source:** Marty Cagan, *Inspired* (2017), Silicon Valley Product Group

Cagan emphasizes that the most common PM failure in ambiguous environments is jumping to solutions before the problem is properly understood. "Fall in love with the problem, not the solution" is the mantra. In practice, this means spending disproportionate time in the problem space — talking to customers, analyzing data, mapping the opportunity — before ever discussing what to build. The PM who can hold the team in the problem space long enough to reach genuine insight, without losing momentum, is operating at a high level of ambiguity tolerance.

**By level:**
- **Level 1-2:** Practice writing problem statements that contain zero solution language. If your problem statement includes words like "we need to build" or "users should be able to," it is a solution masquerading as a problem.
- **Level 3:** You can hold a team in the problem space for days or weeks without losing their confidence. You bring new data, new customer quotes, and new angles to keep the exploration productive.
- **Level 4-5:** You define ambiguous problem spaces for other teams to explore. You write problem frames that are specific enough to be actionable but open enough to allow creative solutions.
- **Level 6:** You identify problem spaces at the business-unit level that no one has articulated yet. You create new product areas by framing problems that cut across existing team boundaries.

## 4. Roadmapping

### 4a. Outcome-Based Roadmaps (Melissa Perri)

**Source:** Melissa Perri, *Escaping the Build Trap* (2018)

Perri argues that traditional feature-based roadmaps ("Q1: Build X, Q2: Build Y") are a symptom of the "build trap" — an organization optimizing for output (features shipped) rather than outcomes (problems solved, metrics moved). An outcome-based roadmap states the business or customer outcome you intend to achieve in each time period, with the specific features remaining flexible and discovered through ongoing research. This approach preserves strategic direction while allowing tactical flexibility.

**By level:**
- **Level 1-2:** Start framing your work in terms of outcomes, even if the organization uses feature roadmaps. Instead of saying "I shipped the notification feature," say "I reduced creator confusion about payment status by 30%."
- **Level 3:** Your roadmap is structured around outcomes, not features. Each quarter has a clear metric target, and the features listed are hypotheses about how to achieve it, not commitments.
- **Level 4-5:** You have converted your team and stakeholders to outcome-based roadmapping. Leadership reviews focus on "are we moving the metrics?" not "did we ship the features?"
- **Level 6:** Outcome-based roadmapping is the standard across your org. You are coaching new PMs and engineering leads on how to operate this way.

### 4b. RICE Prioritization Framework (Intercom / General Industry)

**Source:** Sean McBride, Intercom (2016); widely adopted across the industry

RICE scores each initiative on four dimensions: **Reach** (how many users will this affect per time period?), **Impact** (how much will it affect each user?), **Confidence** (how sure are we about Reach and Impact estimates?), **Effort** (how many person-months will this take?). The score is (Reach x Impact x Confidence) / Effort. RICE is valuable not because the scores are precise, but because the exercise forces PMs to make their prioritization assumptions explicit and debatable.

**By level:**
- **Level 1-2:** Use RICE to prioritize your own backlog. The discipline of estimating Reach and Impact for each item sharpens your product intuition. Share your RICE scoring with your manager to calibrate.
- **Level 3:** Use RICE (or a similar framework) as a communication tool with stakeholders. When stakeholders push for their pet feature, show them where it falls in the RICE ranking and what it would displace.
- **Level 4-5:** You have moved beyond RICE to more nuanced prioritization that accounts for strategic fit, platform effects, and long-term optionality. You use RICE as a starting point, not a decision algorithm.
- **Level 6:** You define the prioritization framework for your org — possibly a customized version of RICE that reflects your specific business context and strategic priorities.

### 4c. Now/Next/Later Roadmap Format (Lenny Rachitsky / General Industry)

**Source:** Lenny Rachitsky, Lenny's Newsletter; widely adopted across product orgs

The Now/Next/Later format replaces date-based roadmaps with commitment-level-based roadmaps. **Now** items are committed and in progress. **Next** items are high-confidence priorities that will follow. **Later** items are strategic intentions that are not yet committed. This format honestly communicates the decreasing certainty of future plans while still providing directional clarity. It reduces the toxic dynamic where a roadmap becomes a set of promises that stakeholders hold the PM to regardless of what is learned.

**By level:**
- **Level 1-2:** Use Now/Next/Later to structure your own work planning. It is a good mental model even before you are responsible for a formal roadmap.
- **Level 3:** Present roadmaps in Now/Next/Later format to stakeholders. Use it to set expectations about commitment levels and explain that "Later" items may change based on what you learn.
- **Level 4-5:** You have established Now/Next/Later as the standard roadmap format for your domain. You use the "Later" column strategically to socialize future direction without over-committing.
- **Level 6:** You use Now/Next/Later at the portfolio level to communicate strategic direction to the executive team and board.

## 5. Trade-offs

### 5a. High-Leverage PM Behaviors (Shreyas Doshi)

**Source:** Shreyas Doshi, Twitter/X threads on "what separates great PMs" (2020-2023)

Shreyas argues that the most important trade-off a PM makes is not between features — it is between activities. Great PMs recognize that their time is the scarcest resource, and they ruthlessly prioritize high-leverage behaviors (setting product direction, making critical decisions, unblocking teams, building conviction) over low-leverage behaviors (writing detailed specs for obvious features, attending status meetings, polishing decks). The trade-off is not "Feature A vs Feature B" but "Do I spend my Thursday defining the strategy for next year or rewriting acceptance criteria?"

**By level:**
- **Level 1-2:** Start noticing where your time goes. Are you spending hours on tasks that do not change any decision? Begin to distinguish between activities that build your judgment and activities that merely keep you busy.
- **Level 3:** You consciously choose high-leverage activities daily. You can say "I am not attending that meeting because this strategy work has higher leverage" without guilt.
- **Level 4-5:** You are coaching your team on high-leverage behaviors. You push back on organizational rituals that consume PM time without creating value.
- **Level 6:** You are redesigning organizational processes to maximize high-leverage PM work across the org.

### 5b. Impact Mapping (Gojko Adzic / General Industry)

**Source:** Gojko Adzic, *Impact Mapping* (2012); widely adopted in product and agile communities

Impact Mapping is a strategic planning technique that connects business goals to deliverables through a four-level hierarchy: **Goal** (the business outcome) -> **Actors** (who can influence this outcome?) -> **Impacts** (what behavior change do we need from these actors?) -> **Deliverables** (what can we build to cause this behavior change?). The power for trade-off decisions is that it makes the chain of assumptions explicit. When you need to cut scope, you can evaluate which deliverables have the strongest causal connection to the goal, rather than arguing about feature preference.

**By level:**
- **Level 1-2:** Use Impact Mapping to evaluate trade-offs in your own backlog. When two features compete for priority, trace each one back to the business goal and compare the strength of the causal chain.
- **Level 3:** Use Impact Maps as a communication tool in trade-off discussions with stakeholders. When someone insists on a feature, ask: "Which actor's behavior does this change, and how does that connect to our goal?"
- **Level 4-5:** Impact Maps become a strategic planning tool. You use them to evaluate entire programs, not just features, and to identify the highest-leverage investment across multiple product areas.
- **Level 6:** You are using Impact Maps to make portfolio-level trade-offs, deciding which product areas to invest in and which to deprioritize based on their connection to business outcomes.

### 5c. Two-Way vs One-Way Door Decisions (Amazon / General Industry)

**Source:** Jeff Bezos, Amazon shareholder letters (2015-2016); widely adopted across the industry

Bezos introduced the concept of **Type 1 decisions** (one-way doors — irreversible or very expensive to reverse) and **Type 2 decisions** (two-way doors — easily reversible). The insight for trade-off management is that most decisions are Type 2, and organizations waste enormous time and energy treating them as Type 1. PMs who can quickly classify decisions and apply the right level of rigor to each make dramatically faster progress. Type 1 decisions deserve deep analysis, broad input, and careful deliberation. Type 2 decisions should be made quickly by individuals or small groups.

**By level:**
- **Level 1-2:** Practice classifying every decision you face as Type 1 or Type 2. You will find that most of your daily decisions are Type 2, and you are spending too much time on them.
- **Level 3:** Use this framework explicitly in team discussions. When a debate is dragging on, ask: "Is this a one-way door or a two-way door? If two-way, let us make a call and move on."
- **Level 4-5:** You apply this framework at the program level. You create fast decision paths for Type 2 decisions (delegate to the closest PM or engineer) and reserve your own attention for genuine Type 1 decisions.
- **Level 6:** You have established decision-speed norms across the org. Teams know which decisions need your input (Type 1) and which they should make autonomously (Type 2).

## 6. Trust & Influence

### 6a. Building Trust as a New PM (Lenny Rachitsky)

**Source:** Lenny Rachitsky, "What separates good PMs from great PMs" (Lenny's Newsletter, 2021-2023)

Lenny identifies trust-building as the single highest-leverage investment a PM can make. His research across hundreds of PM leaders consistently surfaces the same pattern: the best PMs earn trust before they spend it. They do this by being reliable (following through on small commitments), being transparent about uncertainty (never overselling or hiding bad news), and demonstrating genuine curiosity about what engineers, designers, and stakeholders know that they do not. Trust is earned in drops and lost in buckets.

**By level:**
- **Level 1-2:** Focus on reliability. Do what you say you will do, when you say you will do it. If a deadline slips, communicate it immediately. Never let someone discover a missed commitment on their own.
- **Level 3:** Expand trust from reliability to judgment. Stakeholders and engineers trust your reliability — now they need to trust your product instinct. Demonstrate this by making calls that turn out right, and by being transparent when they do not.
- **Level 4-5:** Trust becomes influence. You can propose bold initiatives because you have a track record of sound judgment and transparent communication. People give you the benefit of the doubt on ambiguous bets because you have earned it.
- **Level 6:** You are a trust multiplier. Your endorsement of another PM's proposal carries weight. You use this influence deliberately and responsibly.

### 6b. The Making of a Manager — Trust and Feedback (Julie Zhuo)

**Source:** Julie Zhuo, *The Making of a Manager* (2019)

Zhuo describes trust as the foundation that makes all other management and leadership activities possible. Without trust, feedback is rejected, direction is resisted, and collaboration is transactional. She introduces the concept of "task-relevant maturity" — calibrating your involvement in a team member's work to their experience with the specific task, not their overall seniority. This applies to PMs working with cross-functional partners: you build trust by demonstrating that you understand what they know and what they need, and by calibrating your involvement accordingly.

**By level:**
- **Level 1-2:** Build trust by asking genuine questions. Engineers and designers trust PMs who are curious about their constraints, not PMs who pretend to already know everything.
- **Level 3:** Build trust by giving and receiving feedback well. Zhuo's framework: state the observation, describe the impact, and make a specific request. Accept feedback with the same structure — do not become defensive.
- **Level 4-5:** Build trust by creating psychological safety. People on your team and adjacent teams should feel safe bringing you bad news, disagreeing with your proposals, and admitting uncertainty.
- **Level 6:** Build trust at the organizational level. Your reputation for fairness, transparency, and sound judgment should be known beyond your direct team.

### 6c. Product Sense and Credibility (Lenny Rachitsky)

**Source:** Lenny Rachitsky, "How to develop product sense" (Lenny's Newsletter, 2022)

Lenny defines product sense as the ability to make good product decisions consistently, even with incomplete information. It is the combination of user empathy (understanding user needs and motivations), domain expertise (knowing the market, competitors, and technology), and creativity (imagining solutions that others do not see). Product sense is what earns a PM credibility with engineers — not authority, not process, not Jira ticket management. When you consistently make calls that turn out right, people want to follow your lead.

**By level:**
- **Level 1-2:** Develop product sense by using your own product obsessively, studying competitors, and talking to users weekly. Build a habit of predicting the outcome of product changes before seeing data, then checking yourself.
- **Level 3:** Your product sense is visible to others. Engineers and designers proactively seek your opinion because your instincts have proven reliable. You can articulate why something feels right or wrong, not just that it does.
- **Level 4-5:** Your product sense extends beyond your immediate domain. You can evaluate product decisions in adjacent areas and provide useful input. Your product sense is informed by deep pattern recognition across multiple product cycles.
- **Level 6:** Your product sense shapes the company's product culture. You define what "good" looks like and mentor others to develop their own product sense.

## 7. Communication

### 7a. Narrative-Driven Communication (Amazon / General Industry)

**Source:** Amazon memo culture; Colin Bryar & Bill Carr, *Working Backwards* (2021)

Amazon's memo culture replaces slide decks with 6-page narrative documents. The insight is that narratives force rigorous thinking in a way that bullet points do not. A bullet can hide a logical gap; a paragraph cannot. For PMs, this means that the ability to write a clear, compelling narrative — about a problem, a strategy, a proposal — is a core skill, not a nice-to-have. The narrative format also forces the author to state assumptions explicitly, present evidence, and address counterarguments.

**By level:**
- **Level 1-2:** Practice writing 1-page problem narratives instead of bullet-point feature specs. Can you explain the problem, why it matters, and what you propose in flowing prose? If not, your thinking may not be as clear as you believe.
- **Level 3:** Your written communication is a differentiator. Stakeholders read your documents because they are clear, concise, and well-structured. You can write a compelling 1-page strategy narrative that gets leadership buy-in.
- **Level 4-5:** You define the communication standards for your domain. Your narratives set the bar. You coach other PMs on writing clearly and help them sharpen their documents before sharing with leadership.
- **Level 6:** Your communication shapes company direction. Your strategy narratives, exec updates, and vision documents are reference material for how to communicate product thinking at the organization.

### 7b. Structured Communication for Stakeholder Management (Shreyas Doshi)

**Source:** Shreyas Doshi, threads on PM communication (2021-2023)

Shreyas emphasizes that PMs under-invest in communication as a skill. He distinguishes between **informing** (sharing facts), **persuading** (changing someone's mind), and **inspiring** (creating emotional investment in a direction). Most PMs default to informing — sending status updates, sharing data, listing features. But the highest-leverage communication is persuading (when you need a decision or resource) and inspiring (when you need sustained effort from a large group). Each requires a fundamentally different approach: informing is about clarity, persuading is about addressing objections, and inspiring is about connecting work to meaning.

**By level:**
- **Level 1-2:** Identify which communication mode you are in before writing or speaking. Are you informing, persuading, or inspiring? Tailor your approach accordingly. Most junior PMs default to informing when they actually need to persuade.
- **Level 3:** You can switch fluidly between modes. In a single exec meeting, you might inform (share data), persuade (argue for a resource allocation), and inspire (connect the work to a larger vision).
- **Level 4-5:** You are a deliberate communicator. Every document, email, and presentation has a clear purpose, audience, and desired outcome. You plan your communication strategy for major initiatives the way you plan the product strategy.
- **Level 6:** Your communication is a strategic tool. You use narratives to align the organization, resolve conflicts, and drive strategic change.

### 7c. Common PM Communication Mistakes (Lenny Rachitsky)

**Source:** Lenny Rachitsky, "The most common mistakes PMs make" (Lenny's Newsletter, 2022)

Lenny's research identifies several communication anti-patterns that hold PMs back: (1) burying the lead — starting with context when the audience needs the conclusion first; (2) over-communicating details — sharing everything you know instead of what the audience needs to know; (3) avoiding difficult messages — softening bad news to the point where the urgency is lost; (4) communicating too late — waiting until you have certainty instead of sharing direction and uncertainty early. The cure for all four is the same: know your audience, state your conclusion first, and share early and often.

**By level:**
- **Level 1-2:** Practice "bottom line up front" (BLUF) in all communication. Start with the conclusion or ask, then provide supporting context. Never make your audience read 3 paragraphs to find out what you want.
- **Level 3:** Tailor communication to the audience. Your update to engineering is different from your update to leadership, even if the content is the same. Adjust depth, framing, and emphasis.
- **Level 4-5:** You communicate difficult messages with clarity and compassion. You can tell leadership that a project is failing, a strategy is wrong, or a reorganization is needed without being inflammatory or evasive.
- **Level 6:** You set the communication culture. How you communicate becomes the template for how PMs in your org communicate.

## 8. Community & Mentorship

### 8a. The Manager's Role in People Growth (Julie Zhuo)

**Source:** Julie Zhuo, *The Making of a Manager* (2019)

Zhuo argues that the highest-leverage activity for any leader is helping others grow. This applies to ICs as well as managers — senior ICs who actively develop the people around them multiply their impact far beyond what they can achieve alone. She describes three modes of growth support: **coaching** (helping someone improve at something specific through practice and feedback), **mentoring** (sharing wisdom from your own experience to guide their career), and **sponsoring** (actively advocating for someone's advancement, putting your reputation on the line for them).

**By level:**
- **Level 1-2:** Seek mentors. Be explicit about what you want to learn. Be a good mentee — come prepared with specific questions, follow through on advice, and report back on outcomes.
- **Level 3:** Begin mentoring newer PMs. Share what you have learned, but also be honest about what you are still figuring out. Coaching peers (e.g., helping a designer improve their product thinking) also counts.
- **Level 4-5:** You are a known mentor and coach in your org. People seek you out. You sponsor junior PMs for opportunities — recommending them for visible projects, inviting them to key meetings, advocating for their promotions.
- **Level 6:** You are building the mentorship culture for the PM org. You create formal and informal programs, match mentors with mentees, and ensure that community-building is valued and recognized.

### 8b. Building a PM Community of Practice (General Industry)

**Source:** General industry practice; Etienne Wenger, *Communities of Practice* (1998)

A Community of Practice (CoP) is a group of people who share a concern or passion for something they do, and learn how to do it better as they interact regularly. For PMs, this means creating regular forums — PM critiques, strategy reviews, show-and-tell sessions, reading groups — where PMs learn from each other's work. The key is that these are peer-driven, not management-driven. They work because people choose to participate, and the content is directly relevant to their daily work.

**By level:**
- **Level 1-2:** Participate actively in existing PM community events. Share your work in critique sessions. Ask questions in PM Slack channels. Being a visible, engaged member of the community is the starting point.
- **Level 3:** Organize or co-facilitate community events. Run a PM critique session. Start a reading group. Share a case study of something you learned. You do not need to be the expert — you need to be the convener.
- **Level 4-5:** You are a pillar of the PM community. You have established recurring events, created shared resources, or built traditions that persist even when you are not driving them. You actively bring people together across team boundaries.
- **Level 6:** You shape the PM community at the company level. You define what craft excellence looks like for PMs, and you build the structures (reviews, guilds, apprenticeships) that develop it.

### 8c. Continuous Discovery as a Community Discipline (Teresa Torres)

**Source:** Teresa Torres, *Continuous Discovery Habits* (2021)

Torres emphasizes that discovery is a team sport, not a PM solo activity. The best discovery happens when PMs, designers, and engineers are all talking to customers, running experiments, and sharing insights. Building this shared discovery culture is itself a community and mentorship activity — you are teaching your team a new way of working, not just doing discovery yourself. The PM who can elevate their entire team's discovery capabilities has far more impact than the PM who is the sole discoverer.

**By level:**
- **Level 1-2:** Invite engineers and designers to customer interviews. Share your discovery notes broadly. Make discovery a visible, shared activity rather than something you do alone and report back on.
- **Level 3:** Coach your team on discovery techniques. Help engineers learn to observe user behavior. Help designers formulate testable hypotheses. Create shared rituals like weekly discovery syncs.
- **Level 4-5:** You are building discovery culture across multiple teams. You share frameworks, facilitate cross-team learning sessions, and help teams that are stuck in delivery-only mode start doing discovery.
- **Level 6:** Discovery is embedded in your org's operating model because of the culture you built. New hires learn discovery habits from their teams, not from a training session.

## 9. Scale of Impact

### 9a. Escaping the Build Trap — Outcome Orientation (Melissa Perri)

**Source:** Melissa Perri, *Escaping the Build Trap* (2018)

Perri defines "scale of impact" as the difference between shipping features (output) and moving business metrics (outcomes). A PM who ships 20 features that do not move a metric has less impact than a PM who ships 1 feature that changes the trajectory of the business. Escaping the build trap means measuring your success — and your team's success — by outcomes, not output. This requires the PM to relentlessly connect their work to measurable business results and to be willing to kill or pivot work that is not delivering outcomes.

**By level:**
- **Level 1-2:** Start tracking the outcomes of your shipped features. Did the metric move? By how much? If not, why? This habit builds the judgment you need at higher levels.
- **Level 3:** Your success is measured in outcomes, not features. You can point to 2-3 metrics you moved this half and explain the causal chain from your work to the metric movement.
- **Level 4-5:** Your impact is visible at the business-unit level. The metrics you move are metrics that leadership tracks. You can quantify your team's contribution to company-level goals.
- **Level 6:** Your impact shapes the company's direction. The product strategies you set, the bets you make, and the teams you build produce outcomes that are visible in the company's financial performance.

### 9b. Opportunity Assessment — Quantifying Impact Before Building (Marty Cagan)

**Source:** Marty Cagan, *Inspired* (2017), Silicon Valley Product Group

Cagan's Opportunity Assessment is a lightweight framework for evaluating whether an initiative is worth pursuing before committing resources. It asks four questions: (1) What business objective does this support? (2) How will you know if you succeeded? (3) What problem does this solve for customers? (4) What is the minimum viable approach to test this? The discipline of answering these questions before any initiative scales your impact because it prevents you from wasting months on work that was never connected to a meaningful outcome.

**By level:**
- **Level 1-2:** Use Opportunity Assessment for every new initiative you propose. If you cannot answer all four questions clearly, the initiative is not ready for engineering investment.
- **Level 3:** You use Opportunity Assessment as a standard gating mechanism. No initiative goes to engineering without a clear connection to a business objective and a success metric.
- **Level 4-5:** You use Opportunity Assessment at the portfolio level to evaluate which initiatives across your domain deserve investment. You can compare the potential impact of disparate initiatives on a common basis.
- **Level 6:** Opportunity Assessment is embedded in your org's operating model. Every team uses it, and resource allocation decisions are made based on the relative opportunity size of competing initiatives.

### 9c. Jobs-to-be-Done Framework (Clayton Christensen / General Industry)

**Source:** Clayton Christensen, *Competing Against Luck* (2016); Tony Ulwick, *What Customers Want* (2005)

Jobs-to-be-Done (JTBD) reframes product thinking from "what features does the user want?" to "what job is the user trying to get done, and how well are existing solutions serving that job?" The framework scales impact because it reveals opportunities that feature-level thinking misses. When you understand the full job — including the functional, emotional, and social dimensions — you can identify solutions that are 10x better than the current alternative, not just incrementally improved. These are the bets that produce outsized impact.

**By level:**
- **Level 1-2:** Practice articulating the JTBD for your current product area. What job are users hiring your product to do? What were they doing before? What would they switch to if your product disappeared?
- **Level 3:** Use JTBD to identify opportunities for step-change impact, not just incremental improvement. If you can find an under-served job or a dimension of the job that no one is addressing well, that is where outsized impact lives.
- **Level 4-5:** JTBD analysis informs your product strategy. You can articulate the jobs your product serves across multiple segments, identify which jobs are well-served and which are under-served, and direct investment accordingly.
- **Level 6:** JTBD analysis shapes company strategy. You identify new market opportunities by discovering jobs that the company is uniquely positioned to serve, even if no current product addresses them.

## General Coaching Principles

### Building a Promotion Case

**Evidence Collection (Continuous)**

The most common reason PM promotion cases fail is not lack of impact but lack of documented evidence. Start collecting evidence from day one, not the week before review packets are due. For each criterion in the career framework, maintain a running list of concrete examples — shipped features with measured outcomes, stakeholder feedback quotes, decision documents you authored, mentorship moments, cross-team collaborations. The evidence should be specific ("reduced creator payment failures by 23% in Q3 by redesigning the TIN validation flow") not generic ("improved the payment experience").

Shreyas Doshi advises maintaining a "brag document" — a private running log of your achievements, updated weekly. Each entry should include: what you did, why it mattered, what the outcome was, and who can verify it. This document becomes the raw material for your promo case, your self-reviews, and your 1:1 talking points.

**Narrative Building**

A promotion case is a narrative, not a list of accomplishments. The narrative should answer: "Why is this person already operating at the next level?" The key word is "already" — you demonstrate the behaviors and impact first, then the promotion recognizes what has already happened. Structure your narrative around the career framework criteria, but weave a coherent story: "I started the half owning X. I identified problem Y, which was ambiguous and complex (Criteria 3, 1). I built conviction through Z approach (Criteria 5, 6). I communicated the strategy to stakeholders via A (Criteria 7). The outcome was B (Criteria 9), and along the way I mentored C and built D community resource (Criteria 8)."

Marty Cagan emphasizes that the strongest promotion cases show the PM operating at the next level consistently, not just in one heroic moment. Pattern evidence (3-4 examples of the same behavior across different contexts) is more compelling than a single peak achievement.

### Demonstrating Impact at Higher Levels

**From Execution to Strategy**

At Levels 1-2, impact is measured by how well you execute. At Levels 3-4, impact is measured by the quality of your strategic choices — which problems you chose to solve, which you chose to ignore, and whether those choices turned out right. At Levels 5-6, impact is measured by organizational capability — did you build teams, cultures, and systems that produce excellent outcomes even without your direct involvement?

Lenny Rachitsky's research on what separates great PMs consistently highlights this shift. The best PMs at each level are already demonstrating the behaviors of the next level. They do not wait for permission or a new title to start thinking strategically, building community, or influencing beyond their team.

**Increasing Scope of Influence**

Gibson Biddle describes "increasing scope of influence" as the key unlock for PM career growth. At each level, you should be able to point to impact that extends beyond your immediate team. At Level 3, you influence a product area. At Level 4, you influence a product domain. At Level 5, you influence a business unit. At Level 6, you influence the company. The mechanism of influence shifts from direct execution (Levels 1-2), to strategy and persuasion (Levels 3-4), to organizational design and culture-shaping (Levels 5-6).

### Common Anti-Patterns at Level Transitions

**Level 1 to Level 2: "The Reliable Executor Plateau"**

The anti-pattern is becoming so good at execution that you never develop strategic muscles. You are the PM everyone trusts to ship on time, but no one asks for your opinion on what to build. You are valued for reliability, not for judgment. The unlock is to start having opinions about what to build, not just how to build it. Share your product intuition even when it is not fully formed. Make small bets that demonstrate your strategic thinking.

**Level 2 to Level 3: "The Solo Contributor Trap"**

The anti-pattern is doing everything yourself. You are a strong individual contributor, but your impact is limited to what you can personally execute. You write all the specs, run all the meetings, talk to all the customers, and make all the decisions. The unlock is to start multiplying your impact through others. Delegate discovery to engineers. Let designers run user tests. Trust your team to make decisions in areas where they have stronger context. Your job shifts from doing the work to enabling the work.

**Level 3 to Level 4: "The Domain Expert Ceiling"**

The anti-pattern is becoming the undisputed expert in your domain but failing to demonstrate impact beyond it. You know everything about your product area, but you have not shown that your skills and judgment transfer to new, unfamiliar contexts. You are indispensable in your niche but unproven at broader scope. The unlock is to seek ambiguous, cross-domain challenges. Volunteer for a problem that spans multiple teams. Take on a project in an area where you are not the expert. Demonstrate that your PM skills are generalizable, not domain-specific.

**Level 4 to Level 5: "The High-Output Individual"**

The anti-pattern is producing outstanding work at the Level 4 scope but not building organizational capability. You are a high-performing PM, but the teams and systems around you do not get better because of your presence. When you leave a team, the quality drops. The unlock is to invest heavily in mentorship, community, and process design. Build things that outlast your direct involvement. Hire and develop PMs who are better than you in specific areas. Your impact shifts from what you produce to what the organization produces because of the environment you created.

**Level 5 to Level 6: "The Strategy-Without-Execution Gap"**

The anti-pattern is becoming a pure strategist who is disconnected from execution. You produce brilliant strategy documents and vision narratives, but you cannot point to concrete outcomes that resulted. The gap between your strategic thinking and the organization's ability to execute on it grows wider. The unlock is to close the loop between strategy and execution. Ensure your strategies translate into measurable quarterly goals. Stay close enough to execution to detect when strategy is not landing, and adjust. The best Level 6 PMs combine visionary thinking with operational discipline — they do not choose one at the expense of the other.
